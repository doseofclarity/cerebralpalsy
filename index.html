Cerebral Palsy

Classification · Management · Nursing Care | Adele Pillitteri, 7e

Definition & Epidemiology

Cerebral palsy (CP) – a group of permanent disorders of movement and posture caused by non‑progressive disturbances in the developing fetal or infant brain. It is the most common motor disability in childhood. Incidence ~2‑3 per 1000 live births. In the Philippines, CP is a leading cause of childhood disability.

📊 Key Facts
• Prevalence has remained stable over decades.
• Males are slightly more affected.
• Many children have associated impairments (intellectual disability, epilepsy, vision/hearing problems).
🧠 Important
CP is non‑progressive – the brain lesion does not worsen, but clinical manifestations may change with growth.
⚠️ Early recognition and intervention maximize functional outcomes.

Etiology & Risk Factors

⚠️ Prenatal (70‑80%)
• Congenital brain malformations
• Intrauterine infections (CMV, toxoplasmosis)
• Genetic mutations
• Placental insufficiency
• Maternal thyroid disease, seizures
⚠️ Perinatal & Postnatal
• Prematurity (most important risk factor)
• Intraventricular hemorrhage
• Periventricular leukomalacia (PVL)
• Hypoxic‑ischemic encephalopathy (term)
• Kernicterus (severe jaundice)
• Meningitis, trauma, stroke
📌 Most common risk factor: Preterm birth (especially <28 weeks).

Classification & Clinical Features

TypeCharacteristics% of CP
SpasticIncreased muscle tone, hyperreflexia, clonus, scissoring gait; most common type. Subtypes: hemiplegia, diplegia, quadriplegia.~80%
DyskineticInvoluntary, uncontrolled movements (athetosis, chorea, dystonia). Often due to kernicterus or HIE.~10%
AtaxicPoor balance, uncoordinated gait, intention tremor; often cerebellar damage.~5%
MixedCombination of spastic and dyskinetic features.~5%
Spastic diplegia: Legs > arms, often in preterm infants (periventricular leukomalacia).

Diagnosis & Associated Conditions

  • Diagnosis: Clinical diagnosis based on motor milestones and neurological exam. Standardized tools: GMFCS (Gross Motor Function Classification System). Imaging: MRI brain (detects PVL, malformations, stroke).
  • Associated comorbidities: Intellectual disability (30‑50%), epilepsy (25‑40%), vision problems (strabismus, cortical visual impairment), hearing loss, feeding difficulties/GERD, constipation, hip subluxation, scoliosis.
GMFCS – classifies gross motor function from I (walks without restrictions) to V (wheelchair dependent).

Management & Therapies

🧩 Therapies
• Physical therapy (stretching, strengthening, gait)
• Occupational therapy (ADLs, fine motor)
• Speech therapy (communication, swallowing)
• Orthotics (AFOs, braces)
• Intrathecal baclofen pump for severe spasticity
💊 Medications & Surgery
• Oral medications: baclofen, tizanidine, diazepam
• Botulinum toxin (Botox) for focal spasticity
• Orthopedic surgeries: tendon releases, hip reconstruction, scoliosis correction
• Selective dorsal rhizotomy (SDR) for spastic diplegia
Multidisciplinary team: Developmental pediatrician, physiatrist, PT/OT/ST, orthopedist, neurologist.

Nursing Care & Family Support

  • Promote mobility: Positioning, ROM exercises, use of adaptive equipment (wheelchair, stander).
  • Nutrition/feeding: Assess for dysphagia; upright feeding; consider gastrostomy tube if severe.
  • Bowel and bladder: Constipation management (fiber, fluids, stool softeners); assess for urinary retention/incontinence.
  • Skin integrity: Pressure relief, special mattresses, regular repositioning.
  • Seizure management: Antiepileptic medications, safety precautions.
  • Family support: Provide resources, respite care, counseling; encourage early intervention programs; address sibling needs.
Mnemonic “C.A.R.E.” – Comfort, Activities, Rehabilitation, Education.

Long‑Term Outcomes & Prevention

  • Prognosis: Varies widely – many individuals lead productive lives with appropriate support. Independent walking is achieved by about 50% of children with CP. Life expectancy is near normal for mild/moderate CP.
  • Prevention strategies: • Antenatal: folic acid, avoid infections, manage maternal health.
    • Perinatal: prevention of prematurity (progesterone, cervical cerclage), magnesium sulfate for neuroprotection, therapeutic hypothermia for HIE.
    • Postnatal: prevent kernicterus (phototherapy), head trauma prevention.
🌟 Quality of life: With multidisciplinary care, many individuals with CP attend school, work, and live independently or semi‑independently.
